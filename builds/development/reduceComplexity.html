REDUCE COMPLEXITY

Are overly complex IT systems and processes strangling your business?
Is most of your IT budget spent on keeping the old systems going?

How long since your business fully upgraded its IT systems?

Today, the systems infrastructure in most insurance companies is highly complex.

There may be hundreds of computer systems, manual processes, and spreadsheets in use.

Most are undocumented, and many exist in a virtual dead zone where you only make changes when you absolutely have to.

What happens now?

And when an urgent critical change is required by the business, what happens? Is the current system updated, existing messages extended in the message bus and the database updated? Of course not - that’s way too risky.

Common “solutions” include writing a new system, purchasing new software, or signing up for a SaaS solution. So new system is added to the existing suite, and a future project (which probably won’t happen) is scheduled to integrate the new system to the existing systems and data warehouse.

As a result, the overall system infrastructure becomes over-complicated and the costs to maintain the status quo increase dramatically.

Ever increasing complexity

For the IT team, delivering any real improvement gets increasingly difficult. Meanwhile the business team complain that the IT department can’t deliver anything on time, or within a sensible budget and start looking outside for solutions.

Eventually and in many cases, a drastic step is taken such as outsourcing everything to India, funding a mega-project to bring in a ‘proven’ new system or switching to a SaaS offering.

None of these, of course, improves much in the long run but they do at least lift the fog of inertia for a while and create the illusion of progress.

What are the options?

Unfortunately there are no magic bullets. The process to prune hundreds of systems down to a manageable number and to streamline well entrenched business processes will be the product of a lengthy but well-planned redevelopment project.

Suggestions

Here are my recommendations on how to get started:

Create a rule that for each new system introduced, two old systems must be removed. (Much like my wife’s wardrobe policy - for me this is, not her - I can only buy a new shirt after I throw two old ones out).

Develop a continual review process to identify redundant systems which can be eliminated and processes requiring improvement.

Assign a senior personnel (who has real authority) as sponsor to overcome the inevitable resistance to change and to make critical project decisions.

Benefits

New systems can deliver many benefits, but if the old, less useful systems are not consistently being removed then over time any benefits from the new systems will be far out-weighed by the problems caused by retaining the weaker systems.

The process needs to be funded outside the usual cost/benefit case-driven Capex rules, as most of the changes will be difficult to justify standalone. Initially savings will be piecemeal (e.g. 10 minutes here, 20 minutes there), but eventually these will accumulate to provide real financial benefits.

Intangible benefits include:

Boost in staff morale, as system improvements flow through to day-to-day operations.

Increase in client satisfaction once processes are streamlined, delays and aggravation will be minimised.

So don’t let complexity prevent your business from growth opportunities - get started today.
